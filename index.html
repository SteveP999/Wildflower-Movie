The Story

A small-town voice.
An industry machine.
One unraveling.

Feature Film Drama · Music 117 Minutes Four Acts Original Soundtrack

When a breakout country anthem launches a small-town singer into overnight fame, she must navigate industry pressure, a complicated almost-romance with her producer, and a public downfall — before finding her identity somewhere between who she was and who the world wanted her to be.

Avery Ivey is an unknown singer from a small Texas town, playing modest stages and writing songs that rarely travel beyond county lines. That changes overnight when her breakout anthem "Wildflower" explodes across streaming platforms, turning her into country music's newest sensation almost instantly.

Swept into a world of sold-out shows, label expectations, and carefully crafted image changes, Avery finds herself caught between the artist she's always been and the version of her the industry insists will succeed. As the pressure mounts, so does her reliance on the one person who seems to understand both sides of her world — her producer. What begins as creative trust slowly blurs into something deeper, more complicated, and ultimately unsustainable.

As her sound shifts and the spotlight intensifies, Avery drifts further from her roots. The authenticity that made her rise becomes the very thing she risks losing. When the cracks finally show — onstage, online, and in her relationships — her rapid ascent gives way to a very public unraveling.

Forced to step away from the noise, Avery returns to where it all started. Stripped of expectation and influence, she confronts the choices she made, the voice she silenced, and the person she nearly became.

In the quiet aftermath, Avery doesn't chase the spotlight again — she redefines it. And somewhere between who she was and who the world wanted her to be, she finds something stronger: her own voice.

The Treatment

Four Acts

Act One · 0–25 Minutes

The Bloom

We open on a sun-bleached Texas county fair. Avery Ivey stands at the lip of a plywood stage in mismatched work lights. She's dressed plain. No brand, no polish. She steps to the mic and sings "Wildflower." The drive home with her friend is the last truly free moment she'll have for a while — windows down, radio blaring, two girls who don't know what's coming.

↳"Wildflower" — full performance at the fair
↳"Tonight It Belongs to Me" — on the radio, driving home

Then — overnight — the clip goes viral. The label calls. Nashville comes calling. She says yes before she fully understands what she's agreeing to. Leaving home is harder than she expected. Her mother, suddenly confronted with something she'd always supported but never thought would actually happen. Her father — a quiet man who believed in her more than anyone — full of pride and an equal weight of worry that he won't be able to protect her from what's coming.

↳"Dreams I Packed Away" — bedroom packing montage

Nashville is a lot. She signs. In the production room she meets HIM — Steve Powers. He doesn't try to change her. He asks what the song is actually about. Nobody's asked that yet. She notices. Doesn't say anything about it. Act One closes on the first sold-out show. She thinks she made it. It's actually just the starting gun.

Act Two · 25–55 Minutes

The Illusion

Fame builds fast and weird. A stylist. A brand deck. A social media manager who posts as her. The label is kind but firm: lean into the anthem. You are Wildflower now.

↳"Brand New Me" — Nashville glam montage

But the studio is still hers, at least for now. Late nights with Steve Powers. The work is good — the best stuff she's made. The conversation spills past the music. He's grounded in a way she's forgotten she wanted to be. The air between them starts to feel like something. She doesn't name it. She waits too long.

↳"Lookin' OK" — late-night studio tension with Steve

Then he tells her he's getting engaged. He says it gently, like he knows what he's doing to the room. She says congratulations. She means it and doesn't mean it simultaneously. She goes back to the hotel and writes "Almost Loved You" instead of sleeping.

The midpoint cracks the surface — a panic attack mid-interview, spun as exhaustion. The machine keeps moving. She keeps moving with it. But she felt the gap.

Act Two ends with "Almost Loved You" at a label showcase. She didn't know he'd be there until she walked out. His fiancée is beside him. She's lovely. Avery clocks it in the first two seconds and sings the whole song anyway. He comes backstage. He says it was beautiful. She says thank you. Neither of them will ever say the rest of it.

↳"Almost Loved You" — full showcase performance

Act Three · 55–92 Minutes

The Long Way Down

Burnout accumulates in small ways, then the bigger breaks: a creative blowup with the label, a leaked recording, a story about her that isn't exactly a lie. She performs "Loud Enough" at a live show to lukewarm reception — too rock for the country crowd. The room doesn't know what to do with it. She plays it anyway.

↳"Loud Enough" — live show, wrong crowd, wrong moment

She walks off stage mid-show. Not dramatic. Exhausted. Sets the mic on the stand, walks into the wing, and doesn't come back out.

Then the call comes. Her dad is gone. She walks out of a management meeting mid-sentence. The funeral is small, rural, completely at odds with everything her life has become. She can't cry yet. The grief goes somewhere deeper and she carries it back to Nashville like weight she doesn't know how to set down. Back in Nashville, she becomes more distant and blank. Isolation deepens.

↳"A Soft Place to Fall" — montage of her father
↳"The Long Way Down" — full song over collapse montage

Lowest point. An empty green room. Show was cancelled — or she cancelled it. The rider is still on the table, untouched. She finally cries. Not for the show. For the girl at the county fair who thought she was driving toward something.

Act Four · 92–117 Minutes

Somewhere in the Middle

She doesn't go home. Not exactly. She just gets small. A rented room. A guitar. A notebook she starts filling. She ignores the label calls for a while. Then she answers one, sets a boundary, and holds it. First time she's done that in two years. She notices.

↳"Somewhere in the Middle" — the hopeful turn. The thesis.

New songs come. Quieter, more honest, less formatted. She books 300 seats. Doesn't tell the label. She goes on stage without a setlist. She plays "Wildflower" — alone, guitar only. No lift. No key change. Slower. She plays it like she wrote it: quietly, in the dark, for herself. The room is full of people who came to listen. Not to a brand. Just to her.

The final image isn't a stadium. It's a woman at a microphone, and a room that's paying attention.

↳"Wildflower" (acoustic) — the full-circle closing performance
↳"Hard Truths" — plays over the credits. The truth is, I was never ok.

The Characters

Six people.
One story.

Avery Ivey
Protagonist · Late 20s

Striking in a way that doesn't ask for your attention. Dry humor that surfaces at the wrong moments. Holds everything in until she can't, then writes a song about it. She's self-aware to a fault — she knows when she's performing versus when she's real, and the gap between those two things is what the whole film is about.

Steve Powers
The Producer · Mid–Late 30s

Nashville lifer who still has his integrity somehow. Warm, immediately likeable. Pays attention in a way people mistake for something more. He does feel it — but he met Bailey before Avery existed in his life, and he's not the kind of man who breaks promises. His one moment of private reckoning comes at the showcase. This is not his story. But that moment costs him something.

Female Label Head · Late 40s–50s

She started with integrity and lost it, and she knows exactly what she became. That self-awareness is what makes her interesting — and dangerous. She sees something in Avery she recognizes. She handles it by trying to package it before it gets hurt. No redemption arc. No villain turn. Just a woman who made her peace with the machine a long time ago, watching a younger version of herself refuse to.

Carol & Al Ivey
Avery's Parents · Backstory / Small Roles

Rural Texas salt-of-the-earth. Good people with blind spots. Carol loves Avery completely and has a very specific idea of what that should look like. Al — a quiet man who shows up in the ways he was taught to — hears something in her songs he recognizes and doesn't know what to do with. They're not the reason she's broken. They're part of the context. There's a difference, and the film knows it.
